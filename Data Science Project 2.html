Introduction and Problem Scope¶

The dataset that I decided to analyze for this project is the Titanic Survival Dataset. This dataset includes various columns related to all of the titanic passengers that we currently know about when it crashed into the iceberg and was published about 2 years ago. When I saw this, it was immediately something that I was interested in as history is something that fascinates me. The dataset includes passenger numbers, their names, whether they survived the incident, their passenger class, their sex, their age, the number of siblings and spouses that they had on the trip, how many parents and children they had aboard, their ticket number, the fare that they paid for the ticket, the cabin that they stayed in, and where the passenger embarked from (where they boarded from). The questions that I decided to answer mainly focus on how those certain categories effected the survival rate of the passengers, as there might be some correlation between those columns and what happened to the people that were aboard the ship. In other words, which factors that night had a relationship to the survival rate. This will help get us to a better understanding of why certain people survived in comparison to others and further insights on the crash that might have contributed to the casualities that night. This could benefit stake-holders since it will give them intuition in the historical time period in which the event took place, which could improve our historical knowledge. Along with this, it will allow us to compare the norms during that time period to the ones that we hold now, and assess how the event would have played out if it would have taken place during the time that we live in.

Questions that I Will be Answering¶

  1. What was the true difference between the casualties and the survivors?

So, I took those columns and made some changes to them to make sure that they don't lead to high amounts of inaccuracy in my calculations. For the Age column, I decided to fill the missing values using the median value of all ages. This would be the best decision to make as dropping the values completely would result in an immense loss of data and filling it out with some artibtrary number would lead to higher amount of innacurracy. Along with this, the mean would no be a good option here either as mean is a value that is often skewed easily by outliers, unlike the median.

In [5]:
data["Age"] = data["Age"].fillna(data["Age"].median())

I decided to use the same procedure on the Fare column, as both are numerical data types.

In [6]:
data["Fare"] = data["Fare"].fillna(data["Fare"].median())

For the Embarked column, I decided to fill the mising values up with the location that was the most common between the passengers, which is "S". In this case, both the median and mean would not make sense as this is a string data type, not a numerical one like integers and floats.

In [7]:
data["Embarked"] = data["Embarked"].fillna("S")

Insights Based off of Findings¶

From the findings we have now made based off the questions that we had, we can now frame a better answer as to what really influenced survival the most during the event. As we can see, we saw relationships between a lot of the graphs that we ended up getting. However, a lot of these findings were also influence by the historical time period in which the incident took place, and the way that they decided to react to the situation.

Firstly, we know that there was a pretty obvious relationship between gender and the survival rate, as women had a much higher rate of survival than men. We discussed why this is prevoiusly, as women were often said to evacuate first in the case of an emergency. This is a relationship that can stand by itself and has little indirect relationships with the results of the other graphs.

However, the passenger class, fare, and the location that they embarked from might all be related to one another. As we know from the passenger class, many of the passengers that were part of the higher classes had a higher survival rate, and this makes sense because they were people who, at that time, were seen as people of importance. We can also say that these people were rather wealthy in comparison to the ones that were in the third class.

This shows that the classes also had a role in the fare toll, as people from higher classes are more likely to pay more money for tickets while they are on the ship. The higher ticket could probably be because they wanted to live in a more luxorious suite or a better quality room in general. The room could also be in a more secure part of the ship, which would also lead to more safety when the ship hit the iceberg.

This would also connect with the Embarkation location, as we know that a lot of higher class passengers came from Southampton. Since these people were prioritized in case of an emergency, it would make sense that the death toll for people in that area would be the lowest. Along with this, Cherbourg was the location which had the highest death toll, and at the time it held host to high amount of third class passengers, who had very little access to safety equipment on board.

From these conclusions, we can say that gender defintely had a direct relationship with the survival rate when the event occurred. Along with this, the passenger class, the fare amount, and the embarkation location all had a direct relationship as well but for one similar reason: The norms of social status during the time period in which the event took place.

Now, if this event were to happen today, the survival rate would most likely be a lot different. Since there aren't strict social norms put in place regarding peoples safety, the evacuation system would be much different. It is highly unlikely that they would prioritize one group, and instead treat everyone as equal, doing their best to make sure everyone had access to safety equipment. This would lower the amount of casualities massively, and it would make the above factors that we discussed less important.
